Present Tenses

Present Simple

Basic
Structure: Subject + V1 (base form) + Object
Examples:
  • The sun rises in the east. (= repeated)
  • She has red hair. (= permanent)
  • He always wore a suit. (= a habit)
  • I drink ten cups of tea a day.
  • The train leaves at 10.00 tomorrow. (timetable)
Usage:
  • Permanent states: Facts, general truths, repeated actions
  • Completed actions: When sentence contains numbers referring to 'things done'
  • Future reference: Timetables and scheduled events
  • Subordinate clauses: "If you see Peter, say hello from me."
Important Notes:
  • State verbs rarely use continuous: know, understand, believe, think, mean, love, hate, like, prefer, care, have, own, belong, cost, need, contain, depend
  • Use simple, not continuous, for completed actions with numbers
  • Can refer to future in timetables and subordinate clauses

Present Continuous

Progressive
Structure: Subject + am/is/are + V-ing + Object
Examples:
  • I am working now. (= in progress)
  • What are you doing on your hands and knees? (= in progress)
  • I'm staying with friends until I find a flat. (= temporary)
  • I'm meeting Jo later. (= near future)
  • Don't phone at 8.00. We'll be eating. (= in progress)
Usage:
  • Ongoing activities: Actions in progress at the moment
  • Temporary situations: Limited duration, not permanent
  • Interruptible actions: "We were walking when we were attacked by a bull."
  • Incomplete activities: "I was writing a report on the flight home." (didn't finish)
  • Future arrangements: Near future plans
Important Notes:
  • Duration verbs: live, work - continuous makes them temporary: "Hans is living in London while he's learning English."
  • Short action verbs: lose, break, cut, hit, crash - usually simple, but continuous suggests repeated activity
  • State verbs: Avoid continuous with know, understand, believe, love, hate, have, own, belong

Present Perfect

Perfect
Structure: Subject + have/has + V3 (past participle) + Object
Examples:
  • We have lived in this house for 20 years. (= unfinished past)
  • I have been a teacher for five years. (= unfinished past)
  • You have changed. What have you done to yourself? (= present result)
  • I have lost some weight. (= present result)
  • Have you ever eaten crocodile meat? (= indefinite past)
  • She has never been abroad. (= indefinite past)
Three Main Uses:
  • 1. Unfinished Past: Action began in past, continues to present
  • 2. Present Result: Recent past action, results relevant now
  • 3. Indefinite Past: Unspecified time, focusing on experience
Important Notes:
  • Simple vs. Continuous: Little difference for unfinished past, but continuous suggests more temporary
  • Duration verbs: wait, rain, snow, learn, sit, lie, play, stay - often continuous
  • Short action verbs: start, find, lose, begin, stop, break, die, decide, cut - often simple
  • State verbs: "I've known Joan for years" (not "I've been knowing")
  • Time expressions: recently, before, since January, yet, for months, never, just

Present Perfect Continuous

Perfect Progressive
Structure: Subject + have/has + been + V-ing + Object
Examples:
  • I have been working at the same school all that time. (= unfinished past)
  • I have been doing a lot of exercise. (= present result)
  • I'm tired because I have been working. (= possibly incomplete)
  • Someone has been eating my chocolates. (= there are some left)
  • 'Why are you wet?' 'I have been swimming.' (= activity without details)
  • It has been raining all day. (= duration verb)
Usage:
  • Unfinished Past: Action continuing from past to present
  • Present Result: Emphasizes activity over time period
  • Incomplete Action: Suggests possibly unfinished activity
  • Activity Description: Explains current state without details
Important Notes:
  • Duration verbs: wait, rain, snow, learn, sit, lie, play, stay - often continuous
  • Repeated activity: "I've been cutting wood" vs. "I've cut my finger"
  • State verbs: Avoid with know, understand, believe, love, hate, have, own
  • Simple vs. Continuous: Simple emphasizes completion, continuous emphasizes activity

Past Tenses

Past Simple

Basic
Structure: Subject + V2 (past form) + Object
Examples:
  • Shakespeare wrote plays. (He's dead.)
  • I heard voices coming from downstairs, so I put on my dressing gown and went to investigate.
  • When I was a child, we lived in a small house by the sea.
  • I hurt my back. (But it's better now.)
  • I saw him last night / two weeks ago / on Monday / at 8.00.
Usage:
  • Finished past: Completed actions with no present connection
  • Chronological stories: Actions following each other
  • Past states/habits: "When I was a child, we lived..."
  • Definite past time: With specific time expressions
Important Notes:
  • No present result: "I hurt my back" (but it's better now) vs. "I've hurt my back" (and it hurts now)
  • Definite time: last night, two weeks ago, on Monday, at 8.00
  • vs. Present Perfect: Past Simple = finished past, Present Perfect = connection to present
  • Time expressions: yesterday, last week, in 1992, when I was young

Past Continuous

Progressive
Structure: Subject + was/were + V-ing + Object
Examples:
  • I phoned at 4.00, but you didn't answer. What were you doing? (= activity in progress)
  • The cottage was looking so cosy. A fire was burning in the grate. (= scene setting)
  • I was having a bath when the phone rang. (= interrupted activity)
  • I was reading a book during the flight. (But I didn't finish it.) (= incomplete activity)
  • I was working all day yesterday. (= activity in progress every moment)
Usage:
  • Activity in progress: Before and probably after a time in the past
  • Scene setting: Describing past situation or atmosphere
  • Interrupted activity: "I was having a bath when the phone rang."
  • Incomplete activity: "I was reading a book during the flight." (didn't finish)
  • Duration emphasis: Activity in progress every moment during a period
Important Notes:
  • vs. Past Simple: Past Simple = complete facts, Past Continuous = time and duration
  • Interruption pattern: "I was doing X when Y happened"
  • Scene setting: Often used to create atmosphere before main events
  • Incomplete actions: Suggests activity was not finished

Past Perfect

Perfect
Structure: Subject + had + V3 (past participle) + Object
Examples:
  • Keith was fed up. He had been looking for a job for months, but he had found nothing.
  • He had lost his job and his wife had left him. Since then he had been sleeping rough.
  • I knew his face immediately. I had first met him in October 1993. (= definite time)
  • I recognized her face. I had seen her somewhere before. (= indefinite time)
  • When I had read the paper, I threw it away. (= completed before second action)
Usage:
  • Past in the past: Looking back to action before another past time
  • Story reordering: Tell story in different chronological order
  • Time clauses: Emphasize completion before second action
  • Definite/Indefinite: Can refer to both specific and unspecified past times
Important Notes:
  • Continuous vs. Simple: Continuous = longer actions, Simple = shorter complete facts
  • Time conjunctions: After, as soon as, until, when, before
  • Style: Not every verb needs Past Perfect once time is established
  • Emphasis: Past Perfect emphasizes completion before second action

Past Perfect Continuous

Perfect Progressive
Structure: Subject + had + been + V-ing + Object
Examples:
  • Keith had been looking for a job for months, but he had found nothing. (= longer action)
  • Since then he had been sleeping rough, and he hadn't been eating properly. (= repeated activities)
  • I had been working all day to prepare for the party. (= activity over time period)
  • He had been fighting for the whole of the holiday. (= activity in progress every moment)
  • I had been cutting wood. (= repeated activity over time)
Usage:
  • Longer actions: Activities that lasted a significant time before another past action
  • Repeated activities: Actions that happened repeatedly over a period
  • Duration emphasis: Focus on the time spent on an activity
  • Ongoing activities: Actions in progress every moment during a period
Important Notes:
  • vs. Past Perfect Simple: Continuous = longer/repeated actions, Simple = shorter complete facts
  • Duration verbs: work, sleep, eat, fight - often continuous
  • Repeated activity: "I had been cutting wood" vs. "I had cut my finger"
  • Time period: Often used with "for" + time period

Future Tenses

Future Simple

Basic
Structure: Subject + will + V1 (base form) + Object
Examples:
  • I will work tomorrow. (= prediction)
  • I will have finished the report by 10.00. (= completed before future time)
  • She will live in New York next year. (= future fact)
  • I will lend it to you when I've read the book. (= spontaneous decision)
  • It will snow tomorrow. (= weather prediction)
Usage:
  • Predictions: About future events and situations
  • Spontaneous decisions: Made at the moment of speaking
  • Future facts: Things that will definitely happen
  • Promises/Offers: "I will help you with that."
  • Conditional results: "If you study, you will pass."
Important Notes:
  • vs. Going to: Will = spontaneous, Going to = planned
  • Time expressions: tomorrow, next week, in the future, soon
  • Certainty: Expresses certainty about future events
  • First conditional: "If + present, will + infinitive"

Future Continuous

Progressive
Structure: Subject + will + be + V-ing + Object
Examples:
  • I will be working at 3 PM tomorrow. (= activity in progress at specific time)
  • She will be reading when you arrive. (= activity in progress when something else happens)
  • Don't phone at 8.00. We will be eating. (= activity in progress at specific time)
  • This time tomorrow I will be flying to Paris. (= activity in progress at specific future time)
  • I will be waiting for you at the station. (= activity in progress)
Usage:
  • Activity in progress: At a specific future time
  • Interrupted future: "I will be working when you call."
  • Polite questions: "Will you be using the car tomorrow?"
  • Arranged activities: Pre-arranged future activities
  • Duration emphasis: Focus on the ongoing nature of the activity
Important Notes:
  • vs. Future Simple: Continuous = activity in progress, Simple = complete action
  • Time expressions: at 3 PM, this time tomorrow, when you arrive
  • Polite form: More polite than "Will you use the car?"
  • Arrangements: Often used for pre-arranged activities

Future Perfect

Perfect
Structure: Subject + will + have + V3 (past participle) + Object
Examples:
  • I will have worked here for 5 years by next month. (= completed before future time)
  • She will have finished her homework by 8 PM. (= completed before specific time)
  • I will have finished the report by 10.00. (= completed before future time)
  • By the time you arrive, I will have left. (= completed before your arrival)
  • They will have visited 10 countries by the end of the year. (= completed before year end)
Usage:
  • Completed before future time: Action finished before specific future moment
  • Duration up to future point: "I will have worked here for 5 years by next month"
  • Anticipated completion: Actions we expect to be finished
  • Future in the past: Looking back from a future point
Important Notes:
  • Time expressions: by next month, by 8 PM, by the time, by the end of
  • Duration focus: Often used with "for" + time period
  • Certainty: Expresses confidence about future completion
  • vs. Future Simple: Perfect = completed before time, Simple = action at time

Future Perfect Continuous

Perfect Progressive
Structure: Subject + will + have + been + V-ing + Object
Examples:
  • I will have been working for 3 hours by 5 PM. (= ongoing activity up to future time)
  • She will have been studying for 2 hours when you call. (= duration up to future moment)
  • By next month, I will have been living here for 5 years. (= ongoing duration)
  • They will have been waiting for 30 minutes by the time we arrive. (= duration up to arrival)
  • I will have been learning English for 10 years by 2025. (= ongoing learning)
Usage:
  • Ongoing duration: Activity continuing up to a specific future time
  • Duration emphasis: Focus on how long the activity has been happening
  • Future duration: "I will have been working here for 5 years by next month"
  • Anticipated ongoing: Activities we expect to still be happening
Important Notes:
  • Duration verbs: work, live, study, wait, learn - often continuous
  • Time expressions: by 5 PM, by next month, for 3 hours, when you call
  • vs. Future Perfect: Continuous = ongoing duration, Perfect = completed action
  • Duration focus: Emphasizes the length of time spent on activity

Going to Future

Going to Future

Planned
Structure: Subject + am/is/are + going to + V1 (base form) + Object
Examples:
  • I am going to work tomorrow. (= planned action)
  • She is going to live in Paris next year. (= intention)
  • Look at those clouds! It is going to rain. (= prediction based on evidence)
  • They are going to play football this weekend. (= planned activity)
  • I am going to study harder next semester. (= intention/resolution)
Usage:
  • Planned actions: Pre-arranged future activities
  • Intentions: Personal plans and resolutions
  • Evidence-based predictions: "Look at those clouds! It's going to rain."
  • Imminent events: Things about to happen
  • vs. Will: Going to = planned, Will = spontaneous
Important Notes:
  • vs. Will: Going to = planned/intention, Will = spontaneous decision
  • Evidence predictions: Based on present evidence or signs
  • Time expressions: tomorrow, next week, this weekend, next year
  • Intentions: Personal plans and resolutions for the future
